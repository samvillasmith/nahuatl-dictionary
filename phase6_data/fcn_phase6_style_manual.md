# Flor y Canto Nahuatl Style Manual

**Deliverable 7**  
**Project:** Flor y Canto Nahuatl  
**Subtitle:** Eastern Huasteca Nahuatl for Speech, Modern Standard Nahuatl for Writing  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Date:** 2026-03-27

## 1. Purpose

This manual defines how FCN writes, formats, labels, annotates, and publishes Nahuatl across its major use cases.

It governs:
- neutral prose
- public and broadcast prose
- subtitles
- educational materials
- poetic and classical-facing writing
- annotations and notes

This manual is downstream from the register charter and the orthography manual. It does not replace them.

## 2. Style principles

### 2.1 Register visibility
Every text, excerpt, entry, lesson, or example must have a visible register assignment or inherit one from its container.

### 2.2 No silent mixing
EHN, MSN neutral, MSN public, MSN poetic, and Classical citation may coexist in one publication only if the transitions are explicit.

### 2.3 Readability before ornament in non-poetic domains
In neutral prose, public prose, subtitles, and beginner pedagogy, clarity outranks archaism.

### 2.4 Dignity without artificial inflation
Formal Nahuatl does not need to be made “more literary” by importing classical diction indiscriminately.

### 2.5 Annotation discipline
If a form is unusual, archaic, comparative, restored, proposed, or uncertain, label it.

## 3. Global formatting standards

### 3.1 Register labels
Use the locked FCN register tags in metadata and editorial notes:
- `EHN_colloquial`
- `EHN_formal`
- `MSN_neutral`
- `MSN_public`
- `MSN_poetic`
- `Classical_citation`
- `Comparative_only`
- `Proposed`
- `Deprecated`
- `Needs_review`

### 3.2 Capitalization
Use ordinary sentence capitalization and proper-name capitalization only.

### 3.3 Punctuation
Use standard modern punctuation in public-facing writing. Do not architize punctuation to simulate antiquity.

### 3.4 Numbers
Arabic numerals are acceptable in neutral prose, public prose, subtitles, and educational materials. Literary works may spell numbers out when stylistically appropriate.

### 3.5 Code-switching
Code-switching into Spanish or English must be intentional and editorially defensible. Do not use it as a substitute for missing Nahuatl vocabulary where FCN already has an approved item.

## 4. Neutral prose style (`MSN_neutral`)

### 4.1 Domain
Use in:
- dictionaries
- primers
- general prose
- explanatory writing
- reference materials
- articles intended for broad readability

### 4.2 Tone
Neutral prose should be:
- direct
- clear
- contemporary
- syntactically stable
- lexically conservative

### 4.3 Lexical preference
Prefer:
- approved MSN headwords
- high-frequency vocabulary
- transparent compounds
- consistent terminology once introduced

Avoid:
- unmarked classical-only lexicon
- decorative archaism
- excessive synonym stacking
- unapproved spelling alternation

### 4.4 Sentence design
Prefer medium-length clauses and predictable information structure. Avoid unnecessary embedding where a clearer sequence of shorter clauses will do.

### 4.5 Repetition rule
In neutral prose, repetition is acceptable when it improves clarity. Do not remove useful repetition merely to sound literary.

## 5. Public and broadcast style (`MSN_public`)

### 5.1 Domain
Use in:
- speeches
- websites
- announcements
- subtitles for public institutions when not representing dialect speech directly
- headlines and informational campaigns

### 5.2 Tone
Public style should be:
- clear
- audible
- memorable
- dignified
- accessible to non-specialists

### 5.3 Sentence rule
Prefer shorter clauses than in reference prose.

### 5.4 Vocabulary rule
Use approved MSN vocabulary first. Elevated diction is permitted only when it remains comprehensible in context.

### 5.5 Redundancy rule
Controlled redundancy is allowed in public writing when it improves comprehension in oral delivery.

## 6. Subtitle style

### 6.1 Domain
Use for:
- video subtitles
- lyric subtitles
- educational video captions
- short on-screen translations

### 6.2 Default register
Use:
- `EHN_colloquial` when representing spoken EHN directly
- `MSN_neutral` when producing general written subtitles not tied to a specific dialect performance
- `MSN_poetic` only for songs or ceremonial/literary performances where the elevated register is intentional

### 6.3 Compression rule
Subtitles may compress wording for reading speed, but they may not silently change register.

### 6.4 Line rule
Prefer one clause per subtitle unit. Keep the segmentation syntactically natural.

### 6.5 Punctuation rule
Use punctuation sparingly and functionally. Avoid clutter.

### 6.6 Song rule
When subtitling songs, do not flatten poetic diction into neutral prose unless the subtitle track is explicitly labeled as an explanatory paraphrase.

## 7. Educational style

### 7.1 Domain
Use in:
- beginner lessons
- textbooks
- worksheets
- graded readers
- vocab decks

### 7.2 Default register
Default to `EHN_colloquial` for spoken teaching and `MSN_neutral` for writing instruction.

### 7.3 Safety rule
Every entry, sentence, and lesson item must be marked internally as safe or unsafe for beginner use.

### 7.4 Vocabulary rule
Beginner materials should prioritize:
- high-frequency items
- low-ambiguity glosses
- stable orthography
- concrete semantics first

### 7.5 No silent elevation
Do not introduce `MSN_poetic` or `Classical_citation` items into beginner materials without explicit labels and explanation.

### 7.6 Teaching sequence rule
Introduce one major orthographic issue at a time. Do not stack too many new conventions in one lesson.

### 7.7 Glossing rule
Beginner materials should provide both a gloss and a translation whenever possible.

## 8. Poetic and classical-facing style (`MSN_poetic`)

### 8.1 Domain
Use in:
- songs
- poems
- ceremonial language
- elevated rhetoric
- literary translations

### 8.2 Tone
Poetic style may be:
- denser
- more imagistic
- more parallel
- more allusive
- more rhythmically compressed

### 8.3 Allowed resources
Poetic style may draw from:
- approved MSN-P forms
- classical citation support
- rhetorical formulas
- vocatives
- refrain particles
- elevated lexical alternatives

### 8.4 Required discipline
Even poetic language must remain:
- source-aware
- register-labeled
- orthographically coherent
- editorially attributable

### 8.5 Archaism rule
Classical-looking forms are allowed only when one of the following is true:
- they are already approved as `MSN_poetic`
- they are quoted as `Classical_citation`
- they are explicitly marked as `Proposed`

### 8.6 Boundary rule
A poetic choice does not become neutral prose merely because it sounds beautiful.

## 9. Annotation conventions

### 9.1 Inline tagging
Use inline or metadata tagging whenever a form or text is outside the default register.

Recommended labels:
- `[EHN]`
- `[MSN]`
- `[MSN-P]`
- `[Classical]`
- `[Comparative]`
- `[Proposed]`
- `[Needs review]`

### 9.2 Dictionary entries
Each entry should expose, directly or indirectly:
- preferred form
- register
- variety
- gloss
- source trail
- variant spellings

### 9.3 Example formatting
Recommended example order:
1. Nahuatl text
2. gloss line if appropriate
3. translation
4. register/source note where needed

### 9.4 Source notes
Use concise source notes. Public-facing texts should not be overburdened with internal metadata, but the metadata must remain recoverable.

### 9.5 Uncertainty notes
Use explicit notes such as:
- `orthography provisional`
- `classical support only`
- `speaker validation pending`
- `comparative evidence only`

## 10. What poetic license is allowed

The following are allowed in `MSN_poetic` when intentional and labeled by context:

### 10.1 Parallelism
Balanced or repeated clauses are allowed and encouraged.

### 10.2 Vocatives
Elevated address forms are allowed.

### 10.3 Ellipsis
Ellipsis is allowed when the line remains interpretable.

### 10.4 Refrains
Repeated lines, particles, or formulaic returns are allowed.

### 10.5 Lexical elevation
Approved elevated alternatives may replace neutral forms.

### 10.6 Meter and compression
Poetic compression is allowed, but it may not create untraceable or private spellings.

### 10.7 Controlled archaism
Historically supported forms may appear if they are marked by register and not misrepresented as neutral modern prose.

## 11. What must remain standardized

The following must remain standardized across all FCN outputs unless explicitly quoted from a source:

1. approved orthography of the preferred headword for the active register
2. register labels
3. citation/source traceability
4. variant labeling
5. annotation formatting conventions
6. punctuation and capitalization basics
7. search redirects for alternate spellings

## 12. Prohibited style failures

The following are prohibited editorial behaviors:

### 12.1 Silent classical inflation
Do not make a text look more prestigious by sprinkling in unlabeled classical forms.

### 12.2 Silent register drift
Do not start in neutral prose and drift into poetic diction without marking the shift.

### 12.3 Silent orthographic switching
Do not alternate between legacy and canonical spellings randomly in one edited text.

### 12.4 False spoken authenticity
Do not label reconstructed or heavily normalized prose as direct EHN speech unless it is actually based on spoken evidence.

### 12.5 Decorative obscurity
Do not sacrifice comprehensibility to display erudition.

## 13. Editorial templates by domain

### 13.1 Dictionary entry template
- headword
- register
- variety
- gloss
- notes
- variants
- examples
- source note

### 13.2 Lesson template
- target register
- target vocabulary
- example set
- glosses/translations
- orthography note
- warning label for non-beginner material if applicable

### 13.3 Song/poem template
- text
- register tag
- optional line glosses
- optional neutral paraphrase
- source/literary note if classical material is invoked

### 13.4 Public statement template
- title
- target audience
- register tag
- concise body
- optional audio-friendly rewrite

## 14. Relationship between style and orthography

Orthography decides **how forms are written**.
Style decides **how those forms are selected, arranged, labeled, and deployed**.

Therefore:
- the orthography manual controls grapheme choice
- this style manual controls domain-specific usage

## 15. Locked style decisions for FCN v0.1

For FCN v0.1, the following are locked:

1. Every public text must have a register assignment.
2. Beginner materials default to EHN for speech and MSN neutral for writing.
3. Public prose favors clarity over archaism.
4. Poetic diction is allowed only in explicitly poetic or elevated contexts.
5. Classical material must be labeled when used outside citation.
6. Annotation discipline is mandatory.
7. No silent mixing between EHN, MSN neutral, and MSN poetic.
8. Variant spellings are preserved but one preferred form is selected per register.
