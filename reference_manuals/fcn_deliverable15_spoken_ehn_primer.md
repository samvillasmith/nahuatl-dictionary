## 1. Purpose

This book is the beginner-facing spoken entry point into Flor y Canto Nahuatl.

It is designed to:
- teach spoken EHN first,
- build conversation before literary elevation,
- keep primer material free of hidden classical contamination,
- and provide a clean path from survival phrases to routine communication.

This primer is built on the project's instructional track:
- 23 primer units
- A1, A2, and B1 bands
- contamination status: clean (see Appendix B)

---

## 2. Pedagogical Principles

1. **Speech first.** The primer teaches spoken EHN as the foundational learner path.

2. **No hidden classical contamination.** Classical material is not silently used as ordinary spoken content.

3. **Construction-based teaching.** Learners are taught reusable patterns, not just isolated words.

4. **Dialogue-first progression.** Each unit begins from meaningful speech, not abstract grammar.

5. **Review is mandatory.** Vocabulary, constructions, and dialogues recur through review cycles.

6. **Primer vocabulary is spoken-safe.** Every word introduced at primer level must be attestable in spoken EHN or lesson-derived modern EHN with appropriate evidence status.

7. **Grammar notes are functional.** Notes explain what a pattern does and how to use it — not historical or comparative background.

8. **Drilling is built into each unit.** Six drill types are available; each unit uses at least three.

9. **Glossary is lesson-linked.** Vocabulary is connected to the unit where it first appears.

10. **Register is never hidden.** If a word or phrase carries elevated or classical register, it is explicitly marked.

## 12. Model Unit — Unit 3: What Is Your Name?

**Phase 8 Lesson:** 3 | **Band:** A1 | **Theme:** What is your name?

---

### 12.1 Lesson Overview

This unit introduces the most basic exchange of personal identification in EHN: greeting someone, exchanging names, asking where they are from, and stating your occupation. By the end of this unit, a learner can open a conversation, introduce themselves, and close politely.

---

### 12.2 Communicative Goal

After completing this unit, learners can:
- greet someone and respond to a greeting
- ask and answer "What is your name?"
- ask and answer "Where are you from?"
- state a basic occupation (student / teacher)
- close a conversation politely

---

### 12.3 Core Dialogue

**Context:** Two people — Juan and Paty — meet in Chicontepec.

*Evidence status: constructed_pedagogical — built from attested Lesson 3 vocabulary and constructions.*

---

> A: Piyalli, ¿quēniuhqui motōcah?
> *(Hello, what is your name?)*

> B: Piyalli, na notōcah Paty. Huan ta, ¿quēniuhqui motōcah?
> *(Hello, my name is Paty. And you, what is your name?)*

> A: Na notōcah Juan. ¿Cānin tiēhua?
> *(My name is Juan. Where are you from?)*

> B: Na niēhua Tecomate, Chicōntepēc. Huan ta, ¿cānin tiēhua?
> *(I am from Tecomate, Chicontepec. And you, where are you from?)*

> A: Na niēhua Chicontepec nouhquiya. ¿Tlen ticchīhua?
> *(I am also from Chicontepec. What do you do?)*

> B: Na nimomachtia nāhuatl. Huan ta?
> *(I study Nahuatl. And you?)*

> A: Na nitlamachtia pan caltlamachticān. Cualtitoc, teipan timoittazceh.
> *(I teach at school. OK, we'll see each other later.)*

> B: Cualtitoc, teipan.
> *(OK, later.)*

### 12.6 Grammar Notes

**GN 3-1: Possessed nouns and notōcah**

In EHN, nouns change when they are possessed. The stem -tōcah (name) takes a personal possessive prefix:

| Person | Form | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| 1sg | no-tōcah | my name |
| 2sg | mo-tōcah | your name |
| 3sg | ī-tōcah | his/her name |

In the canonical primer question: *Quēniuhqui motōcah?* / *Na notōcah X.* — notice that the possessed form is used in both question and answer.

**GN 3-2: quēniuhqui**

*Quēniuhqui* means "how" or "in what manner." In the name question *Quēniuhqui motōcah?*, it functions as "What is your name?" — literally, "How does your name go?" This is a high-frequency interrogative. Do not confuse with *tlen* (what/which, for objects and actions) or *cānin* (where).

**GN 3-3: First-person subject prefix ni-**

The first-person singular subject prefix is *ni-* on verbs: *ni-ēhua* (I come from), *ni-momachtia* (I study), *ni-tlamachtia* (I teach). The standalone first-person pronoun is *na* (I). Both appear in the dialogue: *na notōcah Paty* (I, my-name Paty) uses *na* as a topic marker; *na niēhua* (I am from) uses *ni-* on the verb.

*Evidence status: constructed_pedagogical — built from attested Lesson 9 vocabulary and constructions.*

### 13.6 Grammar Notes

**GN 9-1: Plural possession with -huan**

EHN marks the plural of possessed nouns with the suffix *-huan*:
- *nochocho* (my younger sibling, singular) → *nochochohuan* (my younger siblings, plural)
- *mochocho* (your younger sibling) → *mochochohuan* (your younger siblings)

The plural possession suffix -huan applies to sibling terms and other kinship nouns. It is added to the possessed stem (after the possessive prefix).

**GN 9-2: quezqui and the verb ticpiya**

*Quezqui* (how many/how much) takes a possessed noun in the question pattern:
> *Quezqui mochochohuan ticpiya?*

The verb *ticpiya* (you have) is 2sg of *quipiya* (to have / to hold / to keep). Paradigm:
- nicpiya (I have), ticpiya (you have), quipiya (he/she has), ticpiyah (we have)

**GN 9-3: Age expressions with xīhuitl**

Age in EHN uses *xīhuitl* (year) with the verb *quipiya* (to have):
> *Notah quipiya mahtlactli huan chicuēyi xīhuitl.*
> My father has eighteen years. (= My father is 18 years old.)

Note that Nahuatl "has" years rather than "is" years. For teens: *mahtlactli* (10) + *huan* (and) + the additional digit. Example: *mahtlactli huan chicuacē* = 16; *mahtlactli huan chiucnahui* = 19.

### 14.6 Grammar Notes

**GN 10-1: Future tense with -z / -zqueh**

The future tense in EHN is formed by adding *-z* to the verb stem (singular) or *-zqueh* (plural):
- *ticchīhua* (you do) → *ticchīhuaz* (you will do)
- *nipica* (I harvest) → *nipixcaz* (I will harvest)
- *quihtoa* (he/she says) → *quihtoz* (he/she will say)

Use the future when asking about plans or intentions, especially with *mōztla* (tomorrow) or other future time words.

**GN 10-2: axcanah nihueliz — polite declining**

*Axcanah nihueliz* is the standard polite declining formula: "I can't / I won't be able to." Literally: *axcanah* (not-yet / absolutely not) + *ni-hueliz* (I will-be-able). The construction is always followed by a time word: *axcanah nihueliz mōztla* (I can't tomorrow).

*Axcanah* is stronger than plain *ax-* negation. It signals firm inability or firm refusal. Do not use it for mild hedges.

**GN 10-3: pampa — reason clause**

*Pampa* (because / so that) introduces a reason or purpose clause:
> *Pampa nicnequi niquittaz tlan hueliz tinēchpalehuia.*
> Because I want to see you — if you can help me.

Note: *tlan hueliz* (if it's possible / if you can) is a polite framing device for requests and conditions.

### 15.6 Grammar Notes

**GN 17-1: Communal possession with to-**

When something belongs to or is shared by a community or group, EHN uses the *to-* prefix (1st person plural possessive):
- *to-mīllah* (our milpa), *to-tlacualiz* (our food), *to-tēhuāntin* (us / ourselves)

This communal possession is widely used in Nahua community speech. It marks shared resources, shared identities, and collective action. Compare: *nomīllah* (my milpa) vs. *tomīllah* (our milpa).

**GN 17-2: Sequential narration — achtohui and nouhquiya**

*Achtohui* (first / beforehand) and *nouhquiya* (also / likewise / and then) frame sequences of tasks:
> *Achtohui niccuīz cīntli, nouhquiya niquiccuiz etl.*
> First I will take the corn, then I will also take the beans.

This pattern is common in task narrations, recipes, and descriptions of daily routines. *Nouhquiya* does not mean "second" — it means "and also / similarly." The sequence may have more than two steps.

**GN 17-3: Compound verb tlacualchīhua**

*Tlacualchīhua* is a compound of *tlacualli* (food) + *chīhua* (to make/do): "to make food" = to cook/prepare. This pattern of base-noun + chīhua is productive in EHN:
- *tlacualchīhua* (prepare food), *chāntequitl* (household work, from *chān* + *tequitl*).

Recognizing this pattern helps learners build vocabulary by recognizing the components of compound words.

## Appendix A — Drill Template: All Six Drill Types

Using **Ximoquētza!** (Stand up! — FCN-DICT-0004) as the model item.

**A1. Substitution drill**
Model: *Ximoquētza!* (Stand up!)
Substitutes: *Xitlālī!* (Sit down!) / *Xicochī!* (Sleep! / Rest!) / *Xiēhua!* (Get up! / Leave!)
Instruction: Teacher says the cue; student gives the equivalent imperative.

**A2. Response drill**
Teacher says: *Ximoquētza, quēna?* (Stand up, right?)
Student responds: *Quēna, nimoquētza.* (Yes, I'm standing up.)
Vary: teacher uses different imperatives; students respond affirmatively.

**A3. Transformation drill — imperative to first-person present**
Teacher: *Ximoquētza!* → Student: *Nimoquētza.* (I stand up.)
Teacher: *Xitlālī!* → Student: *Nitlāloa.* (I sit down.)
The drill makes the connection between the imperative form (xi- prefix) and the ordinary present tense.

**A4. Question-answer drill**
Teacher: *¿Tlen quichīhua?* (What is he/she doing?)
Student: *Moquētza.* (He/she is standing up.)
Continue with paired actions.

**A5. Sentence building**
Given elements: xi- + mo- + quētza → build: *Ximoquētza!*
Given: xi- + mo- + tlālia → *Xitlālia!* (Sit yourself down!)
Students identify the xi- + mo- + verb pattern as the reflexive imperative frame.

**A6. Short roleplay prompt**
Instructor acts as teacher in classroom. Students respond to instructions:
- *Ximoquētza!* — stand up
- *Xitlālī!* — sit down
- *Ximoquētza!* again — stand up
Short classroom command sequence drills the imperatives in context.
