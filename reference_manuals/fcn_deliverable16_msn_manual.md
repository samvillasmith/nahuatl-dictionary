## 1. Purpose

This manual explains Modern Standard Nahuatl as the project's neutral written norm.

It exists to:
- define the purpose of MSN,
- distinguish it from spoken EHN,
- distinguish it from raw classical citation,
- and provide a stable written standard for education, public prose, and reference work.

---

## 2. What MSN Is

MSN is:
- a teachable written norm
- a neutral reference register
- a public prose standard
- an editorial and educational tool

MSN is not:
- simply spoken EHN written down unchanged
- simply classical Nahuatl respelled
- a poetic register
- a free mixture of sources

---

## 3. Relation to EHN

EHN remains the spoken foundation.

MSN relates to EHN by:
- stabilizing writing,
- reducing speech-local looseness where needed,
- providing a neutral prose norm,
- preserving teachability.

EHN and MSN are therefore connected but not identical.

**Practical distinction:**
- **EHN** = speech, dialogue, primer, conversational pedagogy
- **MSN neutral** = explanation, handbook prose, reference writing, public neutral text

---

## 4. Relation to Classical Material

Classical material supports MSN by:
- providing lexical depth,
- preserving literary inheritance,
- illuminating historical range,
- and supplying citation and comparison material.

Classical material does not automatically define MSN.

When classical material enters MSN, it must do so through:
- normalization,
- adaptation,
- annotation,
- or note-only preservation.

## 5. Orthography

MSN follows the FCN project's orthographic policy. The table below summarizes the key conventions and their relationship to classical spelling.

### 5A. FCN Orthographic Reference Table

| Sound/feature | FCN MSN/EHN spelling | Classical colonial spelling | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| /k/ before any vowel | **k** | c (before a, o, u) / qu (before e, i) | e.g., *kuāwtli* not *cuauhtli* |
| /s/ | **s** | z / ç / soft c | e.g., *siwatl* not *cihuatl* |
| /w/ | **w** | hu (syllable onset) / uh (syllable coda) | e.g., *kuāwtli* not *cuauhtli* |
| /ts/ | **ts** | tz | e.g., *tsin* not *tzin* (in FCN MSN) |
| Glottal stop (saltillo) | **h** | h (inconsistently marked in colonial texts) | Marked consistently in FCN |
| Long vowels | **ā ē ī ō ū** (macrons) | a e i o u (length rarely marked) | FCN marks length editorially where supported |
| /tɬ/ (lateral affricate) | **tl** | tl | Unchanged |
| /ʃ/ | **x** | x | Unchanged |
| /ts/ + /t/ cluster | **tst** | tzt | FCN uses ts consistently |

### 5B. Applied Examples

| Classical form | FCN MSN form | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| cihuatl | siwatl | c→s (initial /s/), hu→w |
| cuauhtli | kuāwtli | c/qu→k, au→āw, hu→w |
| tonatiuh | tōnatiuh | add macron on long ō |
| xochitl | xōchitl | add macron on long ō |
| ximoquetza | Ximoquētza! | add macron on long ē |
| moztla | mōztla | add macron on long ō |

## 6. Public Prose Rules

MSN neutral prose should be:
- clear
- non-poetic
- internally consistent
- stable across educational and public contexts

It should avoid:
- uncontrolled local speech looseness
- uncontrolled literary elevation
- unmarked classical archaism
- mixed orthographic systems

## 7. Style and Examples

### 7A. Example Bank

The following examples are organized by register and grammatical function. All MSN neutral examples are labeled `editorially_normalized` or `constructed_pedagogical` unless otherwise noted — the project's MSN neutral register is being established through this manual itself, not documented from a pre-existing body of MSN neutral prose. This is explicitly acknowledged as the project's evidence posture (see D14 Master Sourcebook).

#### EHN → MSN Neutral (Conversion Examples)

These five examples are drawn from Phase 10 (Deliverable 11, Register Conversion Guide). They illustrate the orthographic and discourse operations applied when EHN spoken material is moved into MSN neutral writing.

| # | EHN source | MSN neutral form | Operation | Note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| E1 | siwatl | siwatl | orthographic_only | Already MSN-aligned; s/w spelling retained |
| E2 | kuawitl | kuawitl | orthographic_only | Canonical w/k spelling retained |
| E3 | akatl | akatl | orthographic_only | Stable neutral headword form |
| E4 | Quēn moxēloa tōnatiuh? | Quēn moxēloa tōnatiuh? | spoken_to_written_stabilization | Lesson-style spoken material preserved in neutral writing where already teachable |
| E5 | Ximoquētza! | Ximoquētza! | spoken_to_written_stabilization | Imperative preserved; no poetic elevation applied |

**Key principle:** Where EHN spoken material is already consistent with FCN orthographic conventions, MSN neutral form is identical. Orthographic normalization is applied where colonial or inconsistent spellings appear.

#### MSN Neutral Model Sentences

These sentences are editorial models — constructed to demonstrate MSN neutral prose style. Evidence status: `constructed_pedagogical`. They are not transcriptions of naturally occurring MSN neutral texts.

**MB-01 — Declarative, language identity**
> *Inin tlahtōlli īpan MSN neutral māquixtia cencah machiyōtl.*
> Morpheme: Inin tlahtōlli īpan MSN neutral mā-quixtia cencah machi-yōtl
> Gloss: this speech in MSN neutral OPT-produce great known-NMLZ
> Translation: "In MSN neutral, this discourse should produce great clarity."
> *Source: FCN Grammar Example Bank, FCN-GRM-B-0001. Editorial model.*

---

**MB-02 — Contrastive declarative, register comparison**
> *EHN ītech tlakakilistli; MSN neutral ītech tlahcuilōlistli.*
> Morpheme: EHN ī-tech tla-kaki-liztli; MSN neutral ī-tech tlah-cuilō-liztli
> Gloss: EHN in-at hearing/speech-NMLZ; MSN neutral in-at writing-NMLZ
> Translation: "EHN belongs to hearing and speech; MSN neutral belongs to writing."
> *Source: FCN Grammar Example Bank, FCN-GRM-B-0002. Editorial model.*

---

**MB-03 — Declarative, daily activity**
> *Na nitlamachtia nāhuatl pan caltlamachticān.*
> Morpheme: Na ni-tlamachtia nāhuatl pan cal-tlamachticān
> Gloss: I 1SG-teach Nahuatl at house-learning-place
> Translation: "I teach Nahuatl at the school."
> *Source: Nāhuatlahtolli lesson corpus (Lesson 3), lesson-derived. MSN-aligned orthography.*

---

**MB-04 — Temporal/sequential, daily routine**
> *Achtohui nitlamachtia, nouhquiya nitequiti pan mīllah.*
> Morpheme: Achtohui ni-tlamachtia, nouhquiya ni-tequiti pan mīllah
> Gloss: first 1SG-teach, also/then 1SG-work in cornfield
> Translation: "First I teach; then I also work in the milpa."
> *Source: Constructed from Lesson 7 and 22 attested vocabulary (achtohui, nouhquiya, tequiti, mīllah). Editorial model.*

---

**MB-05 — Communal possession, agricultural**
> *Pan tomīllah ticcuahcuepaāh etl huan chilli huan cīntli.*
> Morpheme: Pan to-mīllah ti-c-cuahcuepāh etl huan chilli huan cīntli
> Gloss: in 1PL-milpa 1PL-OBJ-cultivate-PL bean and chili and corn
> Translation: "In our milpa we cultivate bean, chili, and corn."
> *Source: Constructed from Lesson 22 attested vocabulary (tomīllah, etl, chilli, cīntli). Editorial model.*

---

**MB-06 — Time reference, travel**
> *Yāhuatzinco niēhualatia pan āltepētl nicchihuaz tequitl.*
> Morpheme: Yāhuatzinco ni-ēhua-latia pan āltepētl ni-c-chihuaz tequitl
> Gloss: at-dawn 1SG-depart-GO to town 1SG-OBJ-do-FUT work
> Translation: "At dawn I will leave for town to do work."
> *Source: Constructed from attested vocabulary (yāhuatzinco Lesson 22, āltepētl Lesson 24). Editorial model.*

---

**MB-07 — Explanatory prose, language description**
> *Miac mācēhualmeh pan Chicontepec quimōmachtih nāhuatl huan quipiyah intlahtol.*
> Morpheme: Miac mācēhualmeh pan Chicontepec qui-mō-machtih nāhuatl huan qui-piyah in-tlahtol
> Gloss: many community-members in Chicontepec OBJ-REFL-study-PL Nahuatl and OBJ-have-PL their-speech
> Translation: "Many community members in Chicontepec study Nahuatl and maintain their language."
> *Source: Constructed from Lessons 3, 11, and language-description vocabulary. Editorial model.*

---

**MB-08 — Food narrative, sequential description**
> *Tlacualchīhua. Achtohui mōtzālan chilli, nouhquiya etl, huan nochi tlacualli quichīhua.*
> Morpheme: Tlacual-chīhua. Achtohui mō-tzālan chilli, nouhquiya etl, huan nochi tlacualli qui-chīhua.
> Gloss: food-prepare. First PASS-grind chili, also bean, and all food OBJ-make.
> Translation: "She prepares food. First the chili is ground, then the beans, and all the food is made."
> *Source: Constructed from Lesson 22 vocabulary (chilli, etl, tlacualli, achtohui). Editorial model.*

#### MSN Neutral → MSN Poetic (Elevation Contrasts)

These three examples show the difference between MSN neutral prose and MSN poetic (elevated) form. They are drawn from Phase 10 (Deliverable 11, conversion examples).

| # | MSN neutral | MSN poetic | Elevation operation |
|---|---|---|---|
| P1 | Noyōllo mitztemoa. | Noyōllo, noyōllo, mitztemoa. | Controlled repetition adds lyric emphasis |
| P2 | Tōnatiuh mōttaz semilhuitl. | Tōnatiuh, titlatlanilia, mōttaz semilhuitl. | Vocative / direct address elevation |
| P3 | Nicāmati in xōchitl. | Nicāmati in xōchitl, nicāmati in cuīcatl. | Parallel pairing using approved elevated imagery |

**What these contrasts show for D16:**
- P1: Repetition belongs to MSN-P, not MSN neutral. A neutral sentence does not double its noun phrases for cadence.
- P2: Addressing tōnatiuh as "you" (titlatlanilia = I ask you) is a poetic elevation. In MSN neutral, tōnatiuh is used as a third-person noun.
- P3: The xōchitl / cuīcatl paired formula belongs to poetic inventory. MSN neutral uses xōchitl as an ordinary noun; the parallel pairing is a literary device.

### 7B. Mini-Paradigms

#### Paradigm 1: Person Prefixes (Intransitive Verbs)

Using tequiti (to work) as the model verb.

| Person | EHN/MSN form | Gloss |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| 1sg | ni-tequiti | I work |
| 2sg | ti-tequiti | you work |
| 3sg | tequiti | he/she/it works |
| 1pl | ti-tequiti-h | we work |
| 2pl | an-tequiti-h | you all work |
| 3pl | tequiti-h | they work |

*Source: Lessons 5, 6, 11 (nitequiti, titequiti confirmed in corpus). 1pl/2pl/3pl forms extrapolated per EHN paradigm.*

**MSN neutral application note:** Person marking is stable across EHN and MSN neutral. No change is made to person prefix forms when moving from spoken to written register. The same forms appear in neutral prose.

---

#### Paradigm 2: Possessive Prefixes with a Noun

Using tequitl (work/task) as the model noun.

| Person | Possessed form | Gloss |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| 1sg | no-tequiuh | my work |
| 2sg | mo-tequiuh | your work |
| 3sg | ī-tequiuh | his/her work |
| 1pl | to-tequiuh | our work |
| 2pl | amo-tequiuh | your (pl) work |
| 3pl | in-tequiuh | their work |

*Morphology note:* When tequitl is possessed, the absolutive suffix -tl drops and the possessive linker -uh is added. This pattern applies to most -tl/-tli nouns.

*Source: Possession paradigm attested from Lesson 8 (possessive marker unit) and Lesson 3 (notequiuh not directly attested but extrapolated per regular paradigm). Paradigm is regular; individual forms await corpus confirmation.*

#### Paradigm 3: Simple Negation

| Construction | Example | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| ax- (prefix, mild negation) | axnicān | not here |
| axquipiya | doesn't have it | |
| axcanah (strong negation) | axcanah nihueliz mōztla | I can't tomorrow (emphatic) |
| axcanah nihueliz | axcanah nihueliz | I cannot / I'm not able to |

*Source: axcanah nihueliz mōztla is directly attested in Lesson 11 corpus. axquipiya attested in Lesson 3 vocab list.*

**MSN neutral application note:** Negation works identically in EHN spoken form and MSN neutral written form. The ax- prefix and axcanah construction are both appropriate in MSN neutral prose.

### 7C. Cross-Register Contrast — EHN vs MSN Neutral

The following table shows where EHN and MSN neutral differ and where they are the same.

| Feature | EHN (spoken) | MSN neutral (written) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core vocabulary | siwatl, tequitl, etl | siwatl, tequitl, etl | Identical where already MSN-aligned |
| Orthography | k, s, w, ts | k, s, w, ts | Identical — FCN uses modern conventions in both |
| Sentence-final particles | present in speech | reduced in prose | EHN discourse markers may be reduced in neutral writing |
| Dialogue markers | Piyalli, Aah | — (prose contexts) | Interaction particles belong to spoken EHN, not neutral prose |
| Person marking | ni-, ti-, etc. | ni-, ti-, etc. | Identical across registers |
| Possessed nouns | notōcah, tomīllah | notōcah, tomīllah | Identical |
| Classical forms | avoided | avoided (except in notes) | Both registers use modern FCN forms, not classical citation |

**Summary:** EHN and MSN neutral share vocabulary and morphology almost entirely. The difference is at the discourse and register level: EHN is organized around speech interaction; MSN neutral is organized around stable, readable prose.

### 7D. Register Cautions

The following cautions are drawn from Phase 10 (what_not_to_do_examples.csv) and the project's governing principles. Each caution is illustrated with a wrong version and a corrected MSN neutral version.

---

**Caution 1: Do not convert classical citation directly into primer dialogue.**
- **Risk:** False everyday naturalness — classical forms can appear modern if respelled, but they may not reflect actual spoken EHN.
- **Wrong:** Presenting *in xochitl in cuicatl* (classical formula) as if it were an ordinary greeting or everyday EHN phrase.
- **Correct:** Keep *in xōchitl in cuīcatl* in literary contexts (MSN-P or classical citation notes) with explicit annotation. In MSN neutral, use ordinary nouns: *xōchitl* (flower) and *cuīcatl* (song) may appear, but the classical paired formula belongs to the poetic inventory.

---

**Caution 2: Do not elevate every neutral sentence into poetic diction.**
- **Risk:** Register inflation — the neutral prose register loses its identity if every sentence acquires lyric elevation.
- **Wrong:** Writing *Noyōllo, noyōllo, ticuīca* in a textbook explanation of possessed nouns.
- **Correct:** In an MSN neutral grammar explanation, write: *Noyōllo tlen ītōcah toyōllotl* (My heart — this is what our heart is called). Save the lyric repetition for MSN-P.

---

**Caution 3: Do not strip source tracking from adapted classical material.**
- **Risk:** Loss of reversibility — once the source note is removed, a classical-derived form may be falsely read as modern attestation.
- **Wrong:** Adapting a Siméon 1885 entry into a modern vocabulary list without noting the classical origin.
- **Correct:** In MSN neutral reference writing, note: *[classical citation: Siméon 1885]* or use the `source_reference` and `classical_citation_form` fields in D18 to preserve the link.

---

**Caution 4: Do not use ceremonial or classical-only items as default spoken forms.**
- **Risk:** Pedagogical distortion — learners given classical-only forms as "the Nahuatl word for X" are learning a historically accurate but practically unusable form.
- **Wrong:** Teaching *tlahtoani* (classical: ruler/lord/speaker) as the ordinary MSN neutral word for "person who speaks."
- **Correct:** Use *tlen quihtoah* (he/she who speaks), *tlahtolli* (speech/language), or describe the classical term with explicit register labeling.

---

**Caution 5: Do not mix EHN spoken forms and classical citation forms in one output without annotation.**
- **Risk:** Register confusion — a reader cannot tell whether the text is teaching modern EHN, documenting classical usage, or mixing both.
- **Wrong:** In a single vocabulary list, presenting *siwatl* (EHN form) and *cihuatl* (classical citation) as if they are interchangeable neutral headwords.
- **Correct:** Present *siwatl* as the EHN/MSN headword with a note: *Classical citation form: cihuatl.* The two forms are cross-referenced but not collapsed.
