# Flor y Canto Nahuatl Register Conversion Guide
## Deliverable 11

**Project:** Flor y Canto Nahuatl  
**Scope:** Register transformation across EHN, MSN neutral, MSN poetic, and Classical citation handling  
**Status:** Draft for internal standardization use  
**Version:** 0.1

---

## 1. Purpose

This guide defines how Flor y Canto Nahuatl converts material across the project's main operative registers:

- **EHN** — Eastern Huasteca Nahuatl spoken foundation
- **MSN neutral** — Modern Standard Nahuatl neutral written norm
- **MSN poetic** — Modern Standard Nahuatl poetic / elevated literary register
- **Classical citation** — Classical Nahuatl preserved for citation, comparison, and literary sourcing

This guide is **not** a free-for-all rewriting license. Its purpose is to establish disciplined, traceable transformation rules so that:

1. spoken material is not falsely classicized,
2. classical material is not falsely treated as everyday speech,
3. poetic elevation is deliberate and marked,
4. note-only citation remains available where conversion would distort evidence.

---

## 2. Governing Principles

### 2.1 EHN remains the spoken base
EHN is the default source for learner-facing spoken content, primer material, dialogue work, and conversational pedagogy.

### 2.2 MSN neutral is not “Classical with modern spelling”
MSN neutral is a teachable written norm. It may use classical support, but it is not created by mechanically respelling colonial material.

### 2.3 MSN poetic is an elevated literary register, not uncontrolled archaism
Poetic elevation must be selective, intelligible, and governed. Repetition, vocatives, refrain particles, and elevated diction are allowed; uncontrolled classical contamination is not.

### 2.4 Classical citation must remain reversible
Whenever classical material is transformed, the original citation form must remain recoverable in notes, source fields, or annotations.

### 2.5 Every transformation should be traceable
Every converted form should be classifiable as one of:
- orthographic normalization
- lexical substitution
- register elevation
- citation preservation
- note-only handling

---

## 3. Register Flow Map

### Allowed standard flows
1. **EHN → MSN neutral**
2. **MSN neutral → MSN poetic**
3. **Classical citation → MSN poetic**
4. **Classical citation → note-only**

### Non-default / discouraged flows
- EHN → Classical citation
- MSN poetic → “spoken EHN”
- Classical citation → EHN conversation
- Mixed-source hybridization without annotation

## 4. EHN → MSN Neutral Rules

### 4.1 Purpose
Use this conversion when spoken EHN material must appear in:
- textbooks
- grammar explanations
- public prose
- dictionary headword normalization
- learner-facing reference writing

### 4.2 Rule classes

#### A. Orthographic normalization
Convert EHN surface spellings to the canonical MSN writing norm.

Examples of the normalization logic already adopted in project work include:
- `k` over legacy `c/qu`
- `s` over legacy `z/ç/soft c`
- `w` over `hu/uh` when representing /w/
- `ts` over `tz`
- `h` for saltillo
- macrons for vowel length where editorially supported

#### B. Spoken-local to written-neutral reduction
Where EHN spoken material contains local spoken forms, contractions, or lesson-specific spellings, convert to the most teachable neutral form **without erasing meaning**.

#### C. Lexical stabilization
Where multiple spoken variants exist, choose the FCN canonical neutral headword and preserve alternates in notes or search fields.

#### D. Syntax and discourse restraint
Do not force spoken discourse markers or conversational looseness into every written sentence. MSN neutral should be readable, stable, and teachable.

### 4.3 What EHN → MSN neutral should preserve
- core meaning
- argument structure
- person/number relations
- temporal/aspectual value where supported
- plain communicative intent

### 4.4 What it may change
- spelling
- some discourse particles
- some local spoken looseness
- some repetition that belongs to speech rather than prose

## 5. MSN Neutral → MSN Poetic Rules

### 5.1 Purpose
Use this conversion when neutral written material is elevated for:
- lyric writing
- poetry
- refrain structures
- ceremonial language
- public literary voice

### 5.2 Main elevation strategies

#### A. Parallelism
Introduce balanced parallel phrasing.

#### B. Controlled repetition
Permit repetition of key nouns, verbs, or vocatives for emphasis.

#### C. Vocative framing
Use vocatives deliberately:
- direct address
- invocation
- communal address
- ceremonial address

#### D. Refrain particles / framing devices
Use refrain particles and repeated openings or closings to mark poetic cadence.

#### E. Elevated diction
Substitute neutral wording with approved elevated diction where intelligibility remains intact.

#### F. Image-bearing phrasing
Permit symbolic or imagistic phrasing when it fits the poetic inventory.

### 5.3 Limits
MSN poetic must remain:
- readable
- internally consistent
- distinguishable from raw classical quotation

Do not elevate by simply piling on archaic material.

---

## 6. Classical Citation → MSN Poetic Rules

### 6.1 Purpose
Use this conversion when classical material serves as a literary source, inspiration, or formal model for modern poetic production.

### 6.2 Approved operations
- orthographic modernization
- lexical modernization where necessary for intelligibility
- retention of high-register imagery
- selective retention of parallelism and vocatives
- explicit annotation when a line is adapted rather than directly quoted

### 6.3 Required caution
A transformed classical line must never be silently passed off as:
- ordinary EHN speech
- neutral modern prose
- unadapted quotation

### 6.4 Annotation expectation
Mark one of:
- adapted from classical
- based on classical citation
- modern poetic rendering of classical source

---

## 7. Classical Citation → Note-Only Rules

### 7.1 Purpose
Use note-only handling when conversion would:
- distort the evidence,
- create false naturalness,
- create false modernity,
- or erase philological value.

### 7.2 Use note-only when
- a form is classical-only
- syntax is strongly citation-bound
- lexical value is historical or literary only
- the line is better treated as an example than as transformable content

### 7.3 Output pattern
Recommended structure:
- original classical citation
- normalized citation if needed
- literal gloss / translation
- note explaining why it is not being converted into MSN poetic or EHN

## 8. Annotated Transformation Examples

### 8.1 EHN → MSN neutral
**Source register:** EHN  
**Operation:** orthographic and neutral-writing normalization  
**Pattern:** preserve meaning, stabilize spelling, reduce speech-local looseness.

### 8.2 MSN neutral → MSN poetic
**Source register:** MSN neutral  
**Operation:** poetic elevation  
**Pattern:** add vocative, parallelism, refrain, or elevated diction without destroying intelligibility.

### 8.3 Classical citation → MSN poetic
**Source register:** Classical citation  
**Operation:** adaptive poetic rendering  
**Pattern:** preserve image and formal force, modernize where needed, annotate adaptation.

### 8.4 Classical citation → note-only
**Source register:** Classical citation  
**Operation:** preserve as citation  
**Pattern:** no forced modernization into active spoken or neutral-modern content.

---

## 9. What Not To Do

1. **Do not back-project classical citation into spoken EHN.**
2. **Do not treat poetic-high diction as ordinary primer language.**
3. **Do not convert every neutral sentence into elevated lyric diction.**
4. **Do not erase source tracking when adapting classical material.**
5. **Do not mix EHN, MSN neutral, and classical forms in a single line without explicit purpose.**
6. **Do not use ceremonial or classical-only items as everyday conversation defaults.**
7. **Do not create “MSN poetic” by merely archaising spelling.**
8. **Do not claim reversibility when a transformation is actually interpretive.**

## 11. Conversion Decision Tree

### Use EHN → MSN neutral when:
- the source is spoken / lesson / primer material
- the goal is neutral writing
- the line should remain teachable and plain

### Use MSN neutral → MSN poetic when:
- the source is already neutral-modern
- the goal is song, poem, performance, or literary-public voice

### Use Classical citation → MSN poetic when:
- the classical source is serving as a literary model
- adaptation is intentional
- annotation will be preserved

### Use Classical citation → note-only when:
- conversion would mislead
- the source is evidence, not target output
- the line is classical-only in force or syntax
