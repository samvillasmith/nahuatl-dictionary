# Flor y Canto Nahuatl — Poetic Nahuatl Manual
## Deliverable 17

**Project:** Flor y Canto Nahuatl  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Status:** Internal/public draft scaffold  
**Scope:** Literary register, lyric usage, and creative practice

---

## 1. Purpose

This manual defines the project’s poetic register and explains how literary Nahuatl should function in FCN.

It is meant for:
- lyricists
- poets
- songwriters
- literary editors
- public-performance writers

---

## 2. Literary Register Definition

The poetic register is:
- elevated
- intentional
- image-bearing
- formally shaped
- connected to classical inheritance without being identical to it

It is not:
- uncontrolled archaism
- primer speech
- ordinary neutral prose
- classical quotation without annotation

---

## 3. Core Literary Devices

### Repetition
Use repetition for:
- cadence
- emotional emphasis
- invocation
- structural framing

### Parallelism
Use parallel phrasing for:
- balance
- ceremonial force
- poetic reinforcement
- lyric organization

### Vocatives
Use vocatives for:
- direct address
- invocation
- communal or devotional address
- public/performative call

### Refrains
Use refrain particles and repeated lines to:
- create memorability
- mark song form
- create emotional or ceremonial return

---

## 4. High-Register Diction

Poetic diction should be drawn from:
- approved elevated lexical inventory
- poetic-high items
- neutral-high items used in elevation
- controlled adapted-classical formulas

Each item should be tagged as one of:
- neutral-high
- poetic-high
- classical-only
- ceremonial
- proposed literary-modern

---

## 5. Annotated Examples

The manual should include:
- plain line
- elevated line
- gloss / translation
- commentary on what was elevated and why
- source note where classical influence is present

---

## 6. Songwriting Exercises

Recommended exercise types:
1. convert neutral prose into elevated lyric form
2. write a refrain from a thematic keyword
3. build a vocative line
4. expand a neutral line into parallel structure
5. adapt a classical formula into a marked modern poetic line
6. compare “too neutral,” “balanced poetic,” and “too archaic” versions

---

## 7. What Not To Do

- do not force classical-only material into every poem
- do not confuse opacity with beauty
- do not remove annotation when adapting classical formulas
- do not let primer-safe spoken material collapse into poetic diction unless deliberate
- do not treat every repetition as automatically effective
